The Idea Validation
Checklist

28 questions to answer before you write a line of code. Most developers build first and validate never. This checklist reverses that — so you only build what's already proven worth building.

How to use this

For each question, click the card that best describes where you stand. Green card = strong signal (+1 point). Red card = warning sign (0 points). Your score updates as you go. An idea with 22+ points is worth building.

01
Problem Clarity
Can you describe the problem precisely from the customer's perspective?
0 / 4
Can you describe the problem in one sentence — from the customer's point of view, not yours?
Good signal

"eBay resellers spend 45 minutes per listing writing titles and descriptions manually."

Red flag

"People need better productivity tools." Too vague to build anything against.

Is this a problem someone experiences repeatedly — not just once?
Good signal

The problem happens weekly or daily. Recurring pain means recurring willingness to pay.

Red flag

It's a one-time event (moving house, planning a wedding). Hard to build subscription revenue on one-time problems.

Do you have personal experience with this problem — or have you directly observed someone who does?
Good signal

You've lived this problem or spent real time with people who have. You understand the nuance.

Red flag

You read about it in a Reddit thread and thought it sounded interesting. Surface-level problem awareness leads to surface-level solutions.

Is the pain acute (it actively costs time, money, or stress) or merely inconvenient?
Good signal

People lose money, hours, or sleep because of this problem. Acute pain has budget attached to it.

Red flag

It's mildly annoying. People tolerate mildly annoying. They pay to fix acutely painful.

02
Evidence of Demand
Where are people already complaining about this — before you exist?
0 / 4
Can you find at least 5 real people publicly complaining about this problem — on Reddit, forums, reviews, or community groups?
Good signal

Specific threads, posts, reviews you can link to. Unprompted public complaints are the purest demand signal.

Red flag

You searched and found nothing. Either the problem doesn't exist or the audience isn't vocal online — both are hard starting points.

Are people currently paying for an imperfect solution — a workaround, a freelancer, or a tool that does 60% of the job?
Good signal

Existing payment behavior proves the problem is real and there's already a budget. You're competing for existing spend, not creating a new category.

Red flag

Nobody is paying anything for any version of this solution. Either the problem isn't painful enough or you've discovered a gap nobody wants to fill.

Have you talked directly to at least 3 people who have this problem — not to pitch, but to listen?
Good signal

Real conversations revealed something you didn't expect. The best validation sessions change your assumptions.

Red flag

Everyone you talked to is a friend or family member. Social proximity makes honest feedback almost impossible.

Is there a measurable, specific outcome people want — not just a vague desire to "be more productive"?
Good signal

"Save 2 hours per week on listing creation." Specific outcomes give you a marketing hook and a product target simultaneously.

Red flag

"Help people do things better." You can't build a product against a vague aspiration.

03
Who Pays
Is there a specific person with a budget and a reason to pay now?
0 / 4
Can you describe your buyer in one sentence — job title, situation, and what they're trying to accomplish?
Good signal

"Part-time eBay resellers selling 20–50 items/month who want to scale without hiring."

Red flag

"Anyone who sells things online." Trying to serve everyone means you'll resonate with no one.

Does this person already spend money on tools or services related to this problem?
Good signal

They have a Canva subscription, a Notion workspace, a VA they pay $200/month. Budget exists. You're asking them to reallocate it.

Red flag

They use only free tools and expect everything to be free. Building for this buyer means competing on zero.

Is there a triggering event that makes someone ready to pay — a specific moment when the pain becomes acute enough to act?
Good signal

"Just listed their 100th item manually and realized they're burning their entire margin on time." Triggering events are your marketing moments.

Red flag

There's no specific moment. The pain is ambient and chronic but never forces a decision.

Would your buyer pay monthly or is this a one-time purchase? And is that consistent with how they currently buy tools?
Good signal

They already pay monthly for 3+ SaaS tools. Subscription behavior is already established.

Red flag

They expect to buy once and own it forever. Subscription pricing will create friction every month.

04
Competition Audit
What exists, what's broken about it, and is that breakage something you can fix?
0 / 4
Have you used the top 2–3 competing products yourself — not just read about them?
Good signal

You can speak specifically to what breaks, what's confusing, and what users complain about in their reviews.

Red flag

You only looked at landing pages and assumed you understood the product.

Is there a specific, articulable reason why existing solutions are failing the people you're targeting?
Good signal

"Existing tools require 10 manual steps. We reduce it to 1 photo." Specific, testable, demoable.

Red flag

"We'll just do it better." Better at what, specifically? "Better" is not a product strategy.

Have you read the 1-star and 2-star reviews of every competing product you can find?
Good signal

The same 2–3 complaints appear repeatedly across reviews. That's your product spec written by your future customers.

Red flag

The negative reviews are scattered and random. No consistent pain means no clear opening.

Is there a reason a well-funded competitor hasn't already solved the problem you're targeting?
Good signal

The market is too small for them, too niche, or requires domain knowledge they don't have. That's exactly where a solo dev can win.

Red flag

There's no good reason. A large competitor could build this in a sprint and you'd be dead. Know the answer before you start.

05
Unfair Advantage
Why you, specifically? What do you know or have access to that others don't?
0 / 3
Do you have domain knowledge in this problem area that would take someone else 5+ years to acquire?
Good signal

You've worked in this industry, built systems for it, or lived the problem. That pattern recognition is your moat.

Red flag

You're building in a domain you know nothing about because you heard it was a good market. You'll make expensive avoidable mistakes.

Do you have direct access to the audience — or can you build access quickly through distribution channels you already have?
Good signal

You're active in communities where your target user hangs out. Or your channel/newsletter already reaches them.

Red flag

You'll have to build the audience from scratch at the same time as the product. That's two hard problems at once.

Can you build a version of this faster than a team at a funded startup — because you know exactly what matters and what doesn't?
Good signal

Your domain expertise means you can cut scope ruthlessly and build the 20% that solves 80% of the problem.

Red flag

You'd need 6 months of research just to understand what to build. Speed is a solo dev advantage — don't squander it on unfamiliar territory.

06
Build Scope
What's the smallest version that proves the core assumption?
0 / 4
Can you describe the MVP in one paragraph — what it does, what it doesn't do, and what assumption it's designed to test?
Good signal

"A tool that takes one photo and generates a complete eBay title, description, and pricing estimate. Tests whether resellers will pay for automated listing copy."

Red flag

The MVP description runs to bullet points across three categories. That's not an MVP — that's a product roadmap. Cut until it hurts.

Can a single developer (you) build and ship the MVP in 4–6 weeks or less?
Good signal

The MVP is tightly scoped enough that it can ship before you lose momentum or circumstances change.

Red flag

The minimum viable version is still a 3-month build. Either the scope is too large or it's not actually the minimum.

Is there a version of validation you can do before writing any code — a landing page, a waitlist, a manual process done for 5 people?
Good signal

You ran the process manually for 3 paying customers before writing a line of code. That's the strongest possible validation signal.

Red flag

Your answer is "no, it requires the software to work." Usually means the scope is too complex or the validation step is being skipped.

Do you have a clear success metric for the MVP — a specific number that tells you whether to keep building or stop?
Good signal

"10 paying users in the first 30 days after launch." Binary. Measurable. Forces a real decision.

Red flag

"See how it goes." Vague success criteria means you'll keep building indefinitely rather than making a real call.

07
Distribution Path
How will the first 10 customers find it — before you build anything?
0 / 4
Can you name 3 specific places — communities, channels, newsletters, forums — where your target user spends time right now?
Good signal

r/Flipping, eBay seller forums, Facebook groups for resellers. Specific enough that you can post there tomorrow.

Red flag

"Social media." Not a distribution strategy. Where specifically? What content? Why would they care?

Do you have a way to reach your first 10 potential users today — without waiting for SEO, ads, or YouTube to work?
Good signal

You can DM 10 specific people right now who have the problem. First users come from direct outreach, not passive discovery.

Red flag

"I'll launch on Product Hunt." Product Hunt is a launch amplifier, not a user acquisition strategy for a niche tool.

Is the product shareable — will users naturally tell others about it, or is it private by nature?
Good signal

Users show their listings to other sellers. The output of your tool is visible in the wild, driving organic word of mouth.

Red flag

The product is entirely private. Growth will require paid acquisition — which changes your unit economics significantly.

If the YouTube channel or community you're building doesn't exist yet — do you have an alternate distribution path for the first 30 days?
Good signal

Yes — specific subreddits, Discord communities, cold outreach to people who've posted about this problem publicly.

Red flag

The entire distribution plan depends on an audience you haven't built yet. Don't tie product launch timing to audience timing.
